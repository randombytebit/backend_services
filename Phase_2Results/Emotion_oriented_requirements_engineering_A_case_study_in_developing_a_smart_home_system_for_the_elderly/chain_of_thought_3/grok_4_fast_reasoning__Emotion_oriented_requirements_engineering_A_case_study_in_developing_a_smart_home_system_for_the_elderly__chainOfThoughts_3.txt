Non-Functional Requirement  
Role: System  
Requirement ID: NFR-01  
Title: Privacy Protection via Non-Intrusive Sensing  
Description: System should use non-intrusive motion sensors for passive monitoring to alleviate elderly users' concerns regarding privacy invasion and the feeling of being overly monitored or stigmatized.  
Citation: Curumsing et al. (2019). Emotion-oriented requirements engineering: A case study in developing a smart home system for the elderly. The Journal of Systems & Software 147 (2019) 215–229. [Section 1, Introduction; Section 3.2.1, Role Model; Section 4.2, SofiHub Emotion-Oriented Requirements Engineering]  
Related Title: IoT-Based Aged Care Monitoring Systems, Ambient Assisted Living Environments  

Threat-Model Reasoning:  
**Let's think step by step.**  
1. **Analyze Privacy Context:** The document emphasizes elderly users' emotional goals like independence and avoiding feelings of being "monitored" or "old/frail," which directly ties to privacy threats from invasive surveillance (e.g., wearables or cameras). This is a Non-Functional Requirement (NFR) focused on minimizing privacy erosion through design choices like non-intrusive sensors.  
2. **Identify Roles:** The 'System' (SofiHub) is responsible for implementing non-intrusive sensing to enforce privacy without compromising monitoring functionality.  
3. **Draft Description:** The description is derived directly from the source, capturing the use of motion sensors to address privacy concerns and emotional threats like feeling controlled or invaded.  
4. **Threat Modeling:** Potential threats include unauthorized visual/audio surveillance leading to data breaches or emotional distress (e.g., loss of dignity). Mitigation via non-intrusive tech reduces attack surface by avoiding high-risk sensors (e.g., cameras), aligning with privacy-by-design principles. No functional trade-offs mentioned, but it supports anomaly detection without privacy violations.  
5. **Map Related Titles:** Relevant to domains like IoT elderly care where privacy-sensitive monitoring (e.g., fall detection) must balance safety and autonomy, such as in EU GDPR-compliant assisted living setups.  

Non-Functional Requirement  
Role: System  
Requirement ID: NFR-02  
Title: Local Data Storage for Privacy  
Description: System should store all sensor data (e.g., motion, door contacts, temperature, humidity) locally within the hub to prevent external exposure and ensure privacy of elderly users' routines and behaviors.  
Citation: Curumsing et al. (2019). Emotion-oriented requirements engineering: A case study in developing a smart home system for the elderly. The Journal of Systems & Software 147 (2019) 215–229. [Section 4.2, SofiHub Emotion-Oriented Requirements Engineering; Section 4.3.1, Data Collection Tools]  
Related Title: Edge Computing in Smart Homes, Privacy-Preserving IoT Data Management  

Threat-Model Reasoning:  
**Let's think step by step.**  
1. **Analyze Privacy Context:** The paper highlights privacy as a core emotional goal ("Privacy secured"), with local storage explicitly used to counter threats like data leakage from cloud syncing, which could expose sensitive activity patterns (e.g., irregular bathroom visits indicating health issues). This is an NFR for data minimization and containment.  
2. **Identify Roles:** The 'System' (SofiHub hub) enforces local storage, limiting access to researchers via periodic dumps only for testing.  
3. **Draft Description:** Pulled verbatim from the source's design rationale, emphasizing local retention to protect against remote breaches while enabling AI learning on-device.  
4. **Threat Modeling:** Threats include network-based interception or cloud breaches (e.g., man-in-the-middle attacks on transmitted data). Local storage mitigates by eliminating transmission vectors, reducing confidentiality risks, though it introduces device-level threats (e.g., physical tampering) that require additional hardening like encryption. Evaluation showed users felt "comfortable about being monitored," validating the approach.  
5. **Map Related Titles:** Applicable to edge-IoT systems in healthcare, where data locality prevents HIPAA/GDPR violations in home-based monitoring for vulnerable populations.  

Non-Functional Requirement  
Role: System  
Requirement ID: NFR-03  
Title: Restricted Access Control for Sensitive Data  
Description: System should restrict access to sensor-captured data to only authorized personnel (e.g., ethics committee-approved researchers) to secure elderly users' personal information and prevent unauthorized viewing of routines or anomalies.  
Citation: Curumsing et al. (2019). Emotion-oriented requirements engineering: A case study in developing a smart home system for the elderly. The Journal of Systems & Software 147 (2019) 215–229. [Section 4.2, SofiHub Emotion-Oriented Requirements Engineering; Section 5.2.5, Privacy and Invasiveness]  
Related Title: Access Management in Elderly Care Platforms, Secure Data Sharing in Socio-Technical Systems  

Threat-Model Reasoning:  
**Let's think step by step.**  
1. **Analyze Security/Privacy Context:** Tied to the emotional goal of "Privacy secured," this NFR addresses insider threats and unauthorized access, as users expressed concerns about feeling "monitored" or privacy violated. Trials confirmed high user comfort (all post-trial scores ≥4 on Likert scale for privacy security).  
2. **Identify Roles:** The 'System' implements access controls, with human oversight (e.g., ethics approvals) for any data dumps.  
3. **Draft Description:** Based directly on the paper's implementation details, focusing on authorization to mitigate emotional and data risks.  
4. **Threat Modeling:** Threats involve unauthorized internal access (e.g., by carers or hackers exploiting weak auth), leading to privacy breaches or emotional harm (e.g., stigma from leaked health data). Role-based access control (RBAC) and ethics gating reduce this, though scalability issues arise in multi-user scenarios (e.g., family access). No breaches reported in trials.  
5. **Map Related Titles:** Relevant to secure platforms in aged care, like telehealth systems requiring fine-grained access to comply with standards such as ISO 27799 for health informatics privacy.  

Non-Functional Requirement  
Role: System  
Requirement ID: NFR-04  
Title: Data Security Against Hacking  
Description: System should secure sensor data against hacking or intrusion to ensure no unauthorized access to sensitive elderly user information, such as routines, anomalies, or emergency data, thereby maintaining user trust and emotional goal of privacy security.  
Citation: Curumsing et al. (2019). Emotion-oriented requirements engineering: A case study in developing a smart home system for the elderly. The Journal of Systems & Software 147 (2019) 215–229. [Section 4.2, SofiHub Emotion-Oriented Requirements Engineering; Emotional Goal: Privacy Secured]  
Related Title: Cybersecurity in Smart Home IoT, Threat-Resistant Aged Care Devices  

Threat-Model Reasoning:  
**Let's think step by step.**  
1. **Analyze Security Context:** The document explicitly states data must be "well secured and no intruders can hack those sensitive information," linking to emotional threats like feeling unsafe or violated. This is an NFR for integrity and confidentiality in an IoT context.  
2. **Identify Roles:** The 'System' (SofiHub) is tasked with anti-hacking measures, integrated into the goal model for emotional reassurance.  
3. **Draft Description:** Drawn from the emotional goal definition and design workshops, emphasizing protection of sensitive data to avoid breaches.  
4. **Threat Modeling:** Primary threats are cyber attacks (e.g., DDoS on hub, firmware exploits on sensors) that could exfiltrate data, causing privacy loss or system failure (e.g., missed anomaly detection). Local storage helps, but requires encryption, secure boot, and network isolation. Trials showed no incidents, with users reporting high privacy security (pre/post scores ≥4), but power failures highlighted resilience needs.  
5. **Map Related Titles:** Pertinent to IoT security in vulnerable-user domains, such as NIST-compliant smart devices for elderly care to prevent exploits like Mirai botnets in home networks.
