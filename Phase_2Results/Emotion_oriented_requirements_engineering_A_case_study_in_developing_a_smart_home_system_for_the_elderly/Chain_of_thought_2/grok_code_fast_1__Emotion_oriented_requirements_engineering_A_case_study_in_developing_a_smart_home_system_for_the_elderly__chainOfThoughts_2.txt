### Architectural Reasoning

- **Component Identification**: The source text describes SofiHub as a smart home system comprising devices and software for sensing, interaction, passive monitoring, and emergency assistance. Core functional capabilities include learning user routines, issuing notifications, detecting anomalies, and providing reminders. These are mapped to emotional goals like feeling safe, independent, and reassured, which serve as quality constraints.

- **Constraint Identification**: Emotional goals (e.g., safe, independent, in control, integrated, comfortable, empowered, privacy secured, reassured) are explicitly identified as non-functional requirements in the emotion model (Fig. 4) and goal model (Fig. 5). They constrain functional capabilities to ensure user acceptance, such as making users feel cared about and reducing loneliness, as evaluated in trials.

- **Functional Logic (FRs)**: Functional requirements are derived from the goal model and SofiHub features, where the system performs tasks like monitoring and alerting. Roles are assigned to the 'System' as the primary component executing these capabilities.

- **Quality Logic (NFRs)**: Non-functional requirements are the emotional goals that must be satisfied for the system to meet user needs, measurable via questionnaires and interviews in trials. Roles are 'Elderly User' for personal emotional goals and 'System' for system-dependent ones, ensuring consistency with the socio-technical design.

- **Domain Mapping**: Functional requirements relate to smart home automation for elderly care, similar to systems in airports or hospitals. Non-functional requirements map to domains like mental health apps or assisted living, where emotional constraints are critical for adoption.
