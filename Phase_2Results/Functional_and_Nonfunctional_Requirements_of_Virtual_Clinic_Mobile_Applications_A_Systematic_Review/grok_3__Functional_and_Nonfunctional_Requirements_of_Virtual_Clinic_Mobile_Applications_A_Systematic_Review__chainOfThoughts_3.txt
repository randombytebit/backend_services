**Threat-Model Reasoning for Virtual Clinic Mobile Applications (VCMA) Requirements**

**Let's think step by step.**

1. **Analyze Security Context:** The document highlights security as a critical non-functional requirement for VCMA, emphasizing encrypted data exchange, secure login methods, and user identity verification. These are essential to protect sensitive patient data and prevent unauthorized access, which could lead to breaches of privacy or data manipulation. This is clearly a Non-Functional Requirement (NFR) focused on safeguarding the system against threats like data interception and identity theft.
2. **Analyze Privacy Context:** Privacy is implicitly tied to security measures such as encrypted data exchange and the ability to delete consultation records. These features ensure that personal health information is protected during transmission and storage, minimizing risks of exposure. This is also an NFR, focusing on data protection and user control over personal information.
3. **Analyze Usability Context:** Usability is identified as a non-functional requirement to ensure the application is user-friendly for diverse users (patients, consultants) with features like personal profile creation and intuitive settings. Poor usability could lead to user errors, potentially compromising security (e.g., misconfigured privacy settings). This is an NFR that indirectly impacts security.
4. **Analyze Accessibility Context:** Accessibility ensures that services like saving appointments and sharing health records are available to all users, including those with disabilities. While primarily an NFR for inclusivity, it also has security implications if access controls are not properly implemented during data sharing.
5. **Analyze Compatibility and Efficiency Contexts:** Compatibility ensures the application works across different systems and records insurance data, while efficiency focuses on financial transaction reporting. These NFRs indirectly relate to security by ensuring seamless operation and accurate data handling, which are critical to maintaining trust and preventing errors that could expose vulnerabilities.
6. **Identify Roles:** The primary role responsible for implementing these requirements is the 'System,' as it must enforce security, privacy, usability, accessibility, compatibility, and efficiency features. Users (patients and consultants) interact with these features but are not responsible for their implementation.
7. **Draft Descriptions:** Descriptions are derived directly from the source document (Tables 1 and 2) to ensure accuracy. For security, specific mechanisms like encryption and verification codes are highlighted. For other NFRs, detailed features from the document are included to reflect the intended functionality or constraint.
8. **Map Related Titles:** Related titles are contextualized to domains where these requirements are critical. For security, telemedicine platforms and health data exchange systems are relevant due to high sensitivity of data. For privacy, remote healthcare apps are pertinent due to the need for data protection in distributed environments. Usability relates to patient engagement tools, accessibility to inclusive health tech, compatibility to cross-platform health apps, and efficiency to healthcare payment systems.
